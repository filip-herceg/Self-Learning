# Acceptance and Commitment Therapy Overview

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) is an evidence-based approach emphasizing psychological flexibility through acceptance of difficult thoughts/feelings, mindfulness, and commitment to values-based action.

## Theoretical Foundations

- Relational Frame Theory (RFT)
- Functional contextualism
- Psychological flexibility model
- Six core processes

## Core Processes (Hexaflex)

### Acceptance
- Willingness to experience difficult emotions
- Defusion from unhelpful thinking patterns
- Non-judgmental observation

### Mindfulness and Present Moment Awareness
- Attention to present experience
- Observation without judgment
- Experiential acceptance

### Cognitive Defusion
- Creating distance from thoughts
- Observing thoughts as thoughts
- Reducing thought dominance

### Values Clarification
- Identifying personal values
- Life domains and valued living
- Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation

### Commitment and Action
- Goal-setting aligned with values
- Behavioral change and activation
- Persistence despite difficulty
- Building meaningful life

### Self-as-Context
- Transcendent perspective
- Observing self vs. content
- Psychological continuity

## Key Techniques

- Metaphors and experiential exercises
- Mindfulness and meditation
- Values clarification activities
- Behavioral experiments
- Defusion techniques

## Applications

### Mental Health Disorders
- Depression and anxiety
- Chronic pain
- Substance use disorders
- PTSD and trauma
- Eating disorders
- Obsessive-compulsive disorders

### Well-Being and Performance
- Performance anxiety (sports, academic, work)
- Work stress and engagement
- Relationship satisfaction
- General life satisfaction

### Special Populations
- Adolescents
- Chronic illness
- Aging and end-of-life
- Occupational health

## Research and Evidence

- Efficacy across disorders
- Mechanisms of change
- Acceptance and mindfulness mechanisms
- Values and commitment effects
- Implementation and dissemination
